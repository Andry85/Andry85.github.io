STOCK MARKET INDICES

Introduction

An index, commonly referred to as a market index (sometimes called a stock index) is a measure of the value of a portfolio or group of stocks, bonds, real estate, or other investments or assets. These days, as the investing industry evolves to cater to an ever expanding demand for more investing products, we see a sophistication, an evolution of index creation. For example, the creation of indices such as the Ethical Investing Indices, which are exclusive to corporations fulfilling socially responsible commitments to programs related to biological, ecological, green, philanthropic criteria, e.g. Dow Jones Sustainability Index.

We also see an evolving trading and trading platform technology. Index Types

Their value is computed by a weighted average of the prices of the stocks which are represented by that specific index. Therefore, the price of each stock within the index is the only criterion upon which the value is computed.

Value Weighted Index / Capitalization Weighted Indices

Another type, different from price weighted is the market value weighted index or a capitalization weighted index which takes into consideration how big a company is when determining value.

Here are a few examples of Market Value Weighted Indices / Capitalization Weighted Indices:

    • NASDAQ-100
    • NYSE Composite
    • FTSE-100
    • Hang Seng Index in Hong Kong
    • RTS Index
    • Russell 2000
    • S&P 500 - Now float-weighted
National Index / Regional Index

An index which tracks a nation’s stock market performance, which naturally correlates investor sentiment is a national index, which are also the most quoted. For example, the American S&P 500, the British ‘footsie’ - FTSE 100; the Japanese Nikkei 224, etc. The FTSE Developed Asia Pacific Index and the FTSE Developed Europe Index are examples of what are labeled as ‘regional’ indexes.

Contracts For Difference (CFD’s)

A contract for difference, commonly referred to as a CFD, is an agreement between two parties to exchange the difference between the opening price and closing price of a contract. Explained in another manner, CFD’s (Contracts for Difference) are derivative products which open up access to trading indices, commodities, foreign currencies without you ever having to actually own the underlying instrument.
